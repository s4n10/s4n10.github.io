Introduction,

The greatest progress in greening the UK has been made in usage and the production of electricity. Coal generation has been largely eradicated, as has oil, to be replaced with gas, wind power and bioenergy. Throughout the UK Homes and businesses are moving into the use of more and more energy efficient products and implementations. However the looking at the motorised transport, it does not seem to be getting any greener continuing to pump out nearly as much climate-harming pollution as it did three decades ago.

According to The Society of Motor Manufacturers & Traders (SMMT) 2019 car registrations report, Sales of electric cars in the UK have risen by 34% since last year. While the electric car sales in china were increased by 154%, however the 34% increase is a significant change from the 11% of the sales increase noted in the year of 2017 to 2018.

An analysis of the latest global sales of electric vehicles found that nearly half the vehicles registered in Norway in the first three months of 2018 were electric (48%). How is UK doing in the electric car race at the moment ?

“China and Norway are exceptional in e-mobility. The lead market remains China, where substantial industrial policy motives are driving battery-electric vehicle technology forward”Stefan Bratzel of the Centre of Automotive Management (CAM).

If china and USA were taken out of the equation for a moment. (The lines can be removed by clicking on the color on the legend representing of each of the respective countries) To focus on Europe, its clear that norway is leading the electric car market in europe by heavy numbers.

Housing the worlds largest public garage for electric cars, Norway is the largest European market for both the Nissan Leaf and the Tesla Model S. Norway’s power grid is exclusively dependant on its hydro power resource. Nearly a third of all new cars sold in the country this year will be a plug-in model – either fully electric or a hybrid – and experts expect that share to rise to as much as 40% next year.

One of the main reasons for this development in norway, is the backing of the Norwegian government offering generous incentives and perks for owning an electric car. Consumers do not have to pay import tax and VAT on plug-in cars, saving thousands of pounds off the upfront cost. Running costs are lower because electricity is cheaper than petrol and diesel, while road tax is reduced – and ultimately dropping to zero next year.

Another reason is that it Electric car owners do not pay the road tolls, ferry fees and city emissions charges that other Norwegians face. Moreover, they can park for free and bypass traffic by driving in some bus lanes.

Looking at the other countries and where the UK stand right now it needs to be clear that, Introducing electric cars to the market might need to be done sooner than expected with the ever rising pollution levels and to keep up with the global adaptation of electric cars. Taking norway as a example it needs to be understood that drastic changes like this must be introduced with major support from the government.

Infrastructure,

According to the data by the English housing survey 2017 only 38% if homes have garages, which would be ideal for a home charging station where consumers can charge their cars overnight make sure their cars are ready to go the next day. The problem arises when targeting the rest of the consumers, according to a report published by the ONS (Percentage of households with cars by income group, tenure and household) 78% of the surveyed houses had one or more car(s) registered to each house. Which means nearly almost all the houses have a car registered to its address.


Decline of House Garages

English Housing Survey 2017: stock condition https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/english-housing-survey-2016-stock-condition

The most common form of parking has been the use of a garage although, overtime the proportion of homes with a garage has steadily decreased, this is yet another contributing cause for the infrastructure failure when introducing electric cars. When more and more people are converting their garages to living spaces, they are forced to park outside the street or use a parking facility. With very limited charging points available all around, in terms of a using an electric car is just not feasible .

Number of Charging stations not ready to meet demands,

In total, there just over 7000 public charging points in the UK, however this is no where near enough to satisfy or to convince a person to go ahead with an electric car purchase, according a analysis carried out by the BBC, to Out of 385 authorities surveyed, only three had 100 or more charging locations. Milton Keynes was found to be leading the way with 138, followed by Westminster with 131 and Cornwall with 115. Overall, two thirds of local authorities were found to have 20 or fewer. Only one charging location was identified in Merthyr Tydfil, Caerphilly, North Dorset and Hinckley and Bosworth.


Conclusion

In order to provide a smooth transition, the charging network must be widespread and more reliable, since lack of charging points happens to be one of the main reasons why consumers are afraid or not wanting to move to an electric vehicle. Also, with the phasing out of the petrol and diesel cars it needs to understood that there are over 18.5million Petrol cars and 12.4 million diesel cars on the UK roads today. Therefore during the upcoming years the electric charging point infrastructure in the UK must be ready to facilitate the massive transition of petrol and diesel vehicles to full-electric.
